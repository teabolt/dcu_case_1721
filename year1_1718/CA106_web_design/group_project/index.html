A primer to card game equipment

Card equipment

About the equipment used for our described card games, first of all we will be using a standard deck of fifty-two French playing cards. If you are wondering what the hell are all of these words, read on.

Playing card details

A playing card is rougly a thin paper or plastic rectangle with a height about twice as big as the width. We will be using fifty-two of those playing cards, a "standard deck"(collection of cards). This deck must be specifically "French". This means that our playing cards will have colours and a bunch of symbols on them. Roughly, we have three "dimensions":

  1. Colour

    There will be twenty-six red cards and twenty-six black cards.

  2. Suits

    "Suits" divide the deck into four sections(like colour divides them into two) - thirteen black "clubs"(similar to how the Irish shamrock looks like) , thirteen red "diamonds"(a rhombus), thirteen red "hearts"(literally hearts), and thirteen black "spades"(almost like hearts but upside down).

  3. Rank

    In each suit there are thirteen cards, each with a "rank" out of the following: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, Jack(J), Queen(Q), King(K) and Ace(A). These ranks are identified by the "pips" on a card. For example the number it shows(two to ten) together with the number of suit symbols. For Jacks, Queens, and Kings we have "face cards". These show some picture as an identifier(of a prince, queen, king, etc)(if these names seem a bit odd, don't worry - they're just historic relics from 15th century German cards and perhaps even earlier times). For the Ace we have a massive symbol of the card's suit. These rank cards exist in each suit, so there are four same rank cards in one deck.

Now these features will only be on one side of a card, meaning that if you flip it over you'll see something generic that all the cards share. That is, when a card is "face down", its colour, suit or rank can not be identified. This will be used for our games extensively.

Advanced card characteristics

For our casual games any card types or dimensions will do("poker" or "bridge" sizes). It's best that you use a French standard deck but if you're advanced you can use any, eg: German, Spanish, etc. playing card type. Yet you'll have to translate our French card terms to your own card type's terms yourself! Some objects may also have multiple terms and we'll pick one to which we are referring to them, eg: a diamond suit is also called a "tile", a Jack(J) is also often called a "Knave". Also in most cases we'll be excluding the infamous "joker" in our explanations.

Card game accessibility

A note regarding cards is that they're quite visual, involving colour, pips, etc. But for those with low vision, braille playing cards do exist. They usually have special features on their surface letting the user sense what kind of a card are they holding. Many card games usually involve not seeing other players' cards, so players won't be put at too much of a disadvantage anyways. For board games accessible equiment may also exist.
