Board Games

Introduction

A board game is a tabletop game that involves pieces or counters and are moved on a pre-marked board according to a set of rules. Some games are based on pure strategy, but many contain an element of chance, while others are purely based on chance, with no element of skill involved. Games usually have a goal that a player aims to achieve.

There are many varieties of board games. Their representation of real-life situations can range from having no inherent theme, like Draughts, to having a specific theme and narrative, like Cluedo. Rules can range from the very simple, like Tic-tac-toe, to those describing a game universe in great detail, like Dungeons and Dragons.

The time required to learn to play a game varies greatly from game to game, but is not necessarily correlated with the number of rules nor the complexity them.

Brief History

Board games have been played in most cultures and societies throughout history. A number of historical sites, artifacts and documents shed light on this.

In 17th and 18th century colonial America, the agrarian life left little time for game playing however card games, bowling and draughts were not unknown.

Modern Board Games

The late 1990's saw a huge growth in the popularity of board games. Around the year 2000 the board gaming industry saw a rapid production of new board games being produced to be sold to an ever growing worldwide audience. In the 2010's, a number of publications called this time to be a Golden Age for board games. They are very popular as there are venues catered just for board games beginning to arise.

Luck and Strategy

Some games, such as chess, are purely based on strategy and skill while other games, mainly children games such as snakes and ladders are purely based on luck as their are no real decisions in these games. There are aloso games that require both skill and luck such as monopoly. A player with luck might win a few games but a player with skill and is able to implement strategies into his play will win more often.
